Computer Engineer

Computer engineers are eseentially engineers involved with computer systems.

engineer noun a professional who designs, builds, or maintains machines or structures.

In other words, a computer engineer designs, builds, and maintains computer hardware — they will often also work with the software aspect of computers to ensure it works as intended.

To do this, some of the skills you might need include programming efficiently, testing programs, webpage development, data analysis, and algorithms. In addition, some more complicated areas of study are artificial intelligence, cybersecurity, and networks.

Requirements

Since computer engineers are a subset of the broader engineer category, a career in this field requires a traditional 4-year university bachelor's degree in computer engineering (there are a few exceptions but you will have more limited job opportunities without an engineering degree). The degree can obtain this at some Canadian universities. They have an application process that involves some combination of your high school marks and extra-curriculars (i.e. competitions, volunteering, jobs). Here are some links for more information:

  • Toronto Metropolitan University
  • York University
  • University of British Columbia
  • University of Toronto
  • University of Waterloo

This is where you will learn the skills you need stated above.

Virtually no one will hire you unless you have built tangible technology-based products and/or developed modular programs. However, this does not mean you will be trapped in an endless cycle of "need experience / need job"! For the actually building part, it is difficult to attain experience without external help (i.e. through a school). This is because you would need to buy or borrow a lot of the components needed to make something, which can be very costly. If you do choose to self-learn these skills, a worthy investment would be a Raspberry Pi or Arduino. Using these "mini-computers", you can make a lot of IoT products (Internet of Things). On the other hand, it is much more accessible to self-learn the programming component, you can follow free online courses — all you need is access to a computer (visit the public library or your school if you do not one). Here are some well-known sources for tutorials:

  • Raspberry Pi
  • Arduino
  • W3 Schools
  • freeCodeCamp
  • Coursera

Some keywords on skills to get you started are

  • Python
  • C++
  • Software Development Process
  • Electrical Engineering (computer components)
  • Version Control: GitHub, Git
  • Algorithms and Data Structures

Another really good way to get experience is through a cooperative education program in university or high school. This allows you to spend a couple of months at a company to gain work experience.
